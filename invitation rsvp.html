Our Heritage

Two cultures,
one celebration

Our wedding weaves together the richness of both Greek and Slovak tradition. Here is a little guide to what you might witness — and be invited to take part in — on the day.

🇬🇷 Greek Traditions

Stefana Στέφανα

The most sacred moment of the Greek Orthodox ceremony. The priest places two flower crowns — connected by a white ribbon — on the heads of the bride and groom, symbolising their union as a new family. The best man (Koumbáros) then crosses the crowns three times between them. Wearing the stefana means you are crowned King and Queen of your own kingdom.

The Dance of Isaiah Χορός Ησαΐα

Three times around the altar. The priest leads the couple in a ceremonial walk — their first steps together as husband and wife. Guests throw flower petals as they circle, blessing the couple with joy and fertility. It is one of the most joyful and moving moments of the Orthodox ceremony.

Krevati Κρεβάτι

Before the wedding, friends and family gather at the couple's new home to "make the bed." The mattress is scattered with rose petals, money, and sometimes a baby is rolled across it — a symbol of luck, prosperity, and fertility. Everyone joins in, laughing and singing.

Money Dance Χορός με χρήματα

During the reception, guests pin banknotes onto the bride and groom as they dance together. It is a generous and lively tradition, seen as a gift to help the couple start their new life — and an excuse for everyone to get onto the dance floor.

Koufeta Κουφέτα

Sugared almonds given to every guest as a wedding favour. The almonds are always in an odd number — typically five — representing health, happiness, fertility, longevity, and wealth. The sugar coating symbolises the sweetness of life; the bitter almond beneath, its challenges. Together, they are inseparable — just like the couple.

🇸🇰 Slovak Traditions

Čepčenie The Capping Ceremony

One of the most emotional Slovak traditions. Shortly after midnight, the bride is seated and married women — led by her mother — remove her veil and replace it with a čepiec, a married woman's cap. It marks the transition from maiden to wife. The ceremony is accompanied by folk songs, and it is not uncommon for both the bride and her mother to shed a tear.

Únos Nevesty Kidnapping the Bride

Do not be alarmed — this one is meant to be fun. At some point during the reception, the groomsmen "kidnap" the bride and hide her away. The groom must find her, and to get her back he may have to complete challenges, sing a song, or pay a symbolic ransom. It is a test of his devotion — and a great source of entertainment for the guests.

Svadobné Koláče Wedding Bread

Elaborately decorated sweet bread, baked by the women of the family in the days before the wedding. The koláče are gifted to guests and symbolise abundance and the warmth of the home. Each loaf is a small work of art — braided, shaped, and adorned with dough flowers and leaves.

Throwing of Coins Hádzanie mincí

As the newlyweds leave the church, coins are thrown around them — and sometimes at them — to bless their marriage with prosperity. Children scramble to collect them, and the couple is showered in wishes for a wealthy and abundant life together.

The First Dance Prvý Tanec

In Slovak tradition, the first dance is not just for the couple — it belongs to the whole wedding. Guests circle the couple during their first dance, singing and clapping, before the dancing opens to everyone. It is a moment of pure collective joy, and a signal that the real party has begun.
